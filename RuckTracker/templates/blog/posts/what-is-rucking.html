What is Rucking?

Rucking is simply walking with weight. Traditionally, this comes from military training where soldiers carry a loaded rucksack for distance. Today, rucking is a highly effective, low-impact fitness activity that improves endurance, strength, and cardiovascular health—without the pounding of running.

Benefits of Rucking

  • Low-impact calorie burn: Burn more than walking, often comparable to easy running depending on load and pace.
  • Builds strength and posture: Engages posterior chain, core, and stabilizers.
  • Time-efficient: Add weight to turn a normal walk into a powerful workout.
  • Great outdoors: Explore trails, parks, and neighborhoods while training.

How to Start Rucking

  1. Choose a pack: Any sturdy backpack works to start. Dedicated ruck packs are more comfortable for heavier loads.
  2. Start light: 5–10% of bodyweight for beginners. Focus on form before adding weight.
  3. Pick safe terrain: Flat paths first, then add hills or trails as you adapt.
  4. Walk tall: Neutral spine, shoulders back, core engaged. Shorter, quick steps.

Technique Tips

  • Distribute load high and close to your back.
  • Tighten shoulder straps to reduce bounce.
  • Use a hip belt for longer distances to share load.
  • Progress slowly: increase only one variable at a time (distance, weight, or pace).

Gear Basics

  • Backpack: Padded straps and frame sheet help a lot.
  • Weight: Ruck plates, dumbbells, sandbags, or water jugs.
  • Shoes: Supportive walking/hiking shoes. Trail shoes for off-road.
  • Socks: Wool or synthetic to minimize blisters.

Beginner Plan (2–3x/week)

  • Week 1–2: 30–40 min, light weight, easy pace
  • Week 3–4: 40–50 min, same weight, varied terrain
  • Week 5–6: Keep time, add a small weight increase

Safety and Recovery

  • Stop if you feel sharp pain. Fatigue is normal; pain is not.
  • Stretch calves/hip flexors. Strengthen glutes/core to support better form.
  • Hydrate and fuel—rucking is deceptively demanding.
